From 2001 until 2006 I studied computer science at the Vrije Universiteit in Amsterdam.

## Choosing a study

I was fond of computers and what you could to with them from a young age, so computer science seemed the logical choice. I considered some related fields, such as business informatics and artificial intelligence, but these did not seem like a right fit. At the time, artificial intelligence was mostly statistics and not the exciting field it is today. I bought a textbook on artificial intelligence, and I was severely underwhelmed by its contents. Instead of making computers think for themselves, it was mostly graphs with red and blue dots and methods to separate the two groups using statistics. So I started computer science.

## Starting computer science

The university was in Amsterdam, which has a vibrant student environment. Instead, I kept living with my parents in Almere. The student live in the capital city didn't really appeal to me.

I tried it a bit at introduction week. This was not even of a fraternity but of the student body. I stayed at a student's dorm room and went partying and drinking. I smoked a joint and passed out in a bar. After that I considered I had tried and failed, and I went home.

The first year of study took some getting used to. At first I bought all the books that were recommended for the various courses, but some remained in the plastic they came in, never to be opened. For some courses the books were essential, and for other courses they were supplemental. The books were quite expensive, so I did without if at all possible.

Many people dropped out in the first year. I am not sure why, but I think less then half remained after the first year.

Students were responsible for their own schedules, picking which courses to follow and attending the courses and the exams. This was a strain on my anxiety about planning. I am still sometimes having nightmares that I missed an exam or that I didn't check the online schedule in a while, and perhaps I have to be at university.

The first year they explained what a computer was and how it worked. It was all very basic and slow, and I became bored.

After handing in exams, you would have to fill out your personal details on a form, such as your student number and name, and sign it. The boxes on the form were very small, and I have a long name. I used to sign "STLangkemper", but because that did not fit in the boxes I would sign "STLangke". That is still my signature to this day.

One teacher said during lectures that anyone who was bored should contact him, so I send him an email and he suggested some self-study material. He suggested reading [*The Mythical Man-Month* by Fred Brooks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Mythical_Man-Month), and it was pretty insightful.

I also started some side projects, such as building my own still (to distill liquer) and building a flamethrower.

I picked up an extra math course on topology. It was interesting, but I lacked basic math fundamentals to pass the test, such as how to write a formal proof. Also, this extra course overlapped with some of my regular courses, making it difficult to do both.

## Sudden technically difficuly

In the second year, it suddenly became challenging. Especially the theoretical computer science courses were really in-depth and needed serious study to pass. I was used to passing everything with little effort, and I was wound up in my side projects and my overlapping math course. When the study suddenly became difficult, I adapted too slow and I failed some courses.

## Intermezzo: internship

I thought it was a good idea to get some field experience, so I applied for an internship at a software developing company. Yes-Co was a small company that created a web application for realtors in PHP. The internship was not necessary for my study, I just did it to get some actual experience. I was not really clear when I applied to the company. I didn't really lie, but I said I was studying computer science and wanted to apply for an internship, implying a formal relationship between the two that didn't exist. At one time they asked whether my supervisor would visit, or whether they would need to fill in some form, but I said they didn't have to and that was that. They were very happy with me and I transitioned into a paid position. I learned a lot there and it really gave me a great start at the job market.

## Masters in internet & webtechnology

In the fourth year I started my masters in internet and webtechnology. This was after the dotcom bubble, but before enshittification and platformization.

## People

[Wan Fokkink](https://www.cs.vu.nl/~wanf/)

The head of the department, [Maarten van Steen](https://www.distributed-systems.net/index.php/me/), was an inspirational man that saw possibilities yet to come on the internet. For example, applications would dynamically interface with third-party webservices. This only recently became a reality, with LLMs. Global discovery and interfacing for software didn't came to realization, but I thought it was visionary of Maarten nonetheless.

There was also a postdoc, [Guillaume Pierre](http://www.globule.org/), who was working on something like a decentralized CDN. Content would automatically be distributed between nodes in the overlay network, and clients would be redirected to nodes close to them. They needed nodes for the decentralized overlay network, and started a contest for students: you could win an iPod or iPad or something if you tried out this CDN software. I was the only one of the class who gave it a try, and unfortunately that meant the contest was cancelled, instead of victory by default for me. I thought it was interesting that I was the only one with a home server who was willing to try out this new technology, in a class of internet & webtech students.
